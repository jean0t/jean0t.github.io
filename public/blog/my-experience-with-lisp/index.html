My Experience With Lisp

18 Mar, 2025

Lisp is the second oldest high level language, younger only to Fortran, which was an inspiration for its creation. And as many people I was baffled by its parenthesis and syntax, I thought it was counter intuitive and possibly hard to use compared to traditional C like langauges.
But! After engaging in learning it for a few days I learned its true value, I used it to practice algorithms like sorting and searching and one of its values was to allow me to understand recursion better, before it was an abstract context but now it is a concrete thing that I use if I need to.

How it works

Lisp is a confusion of parenthesis, at least that is what you will see when you get a lisp code to read, but in the middle of these parenthesis lies a coherent syntax and ellegant solutions to programming problems.
It works with the use of S-Expressions, that is basically a beautiful name to expressions that looks like:

(format t "Hello world")

(<function> <arguments>)

And there are concepts like, atoms, cons, etc.
One thing that in my opinion is the most important to know about lisp, is that it isnt a language like javascript, java or C#, but a concept that can be implemented in different ways. Knowing that will make it less strange when you hear about ’lisp dialects’, they are basically the lisp concept applied in some ways, some more different than others, but that is it.

My experience

Lisp was my go-to after I wanted to try something different, after 3 days of study I had already long lost the fear I felt over its parenthesis and things started making sense to me. I am not proficient yet to built a DE in lisp yet, but I am confident that I can at least make some web applications through hunchtooth.
Ive learned about other lisp dialects and the one I am seeing the most after the starting in this journey with common lisp is the guile implementation of scheme (Scheme is considered lisp 1 while common lisp is considered lisp 2). Guile is really nice, but its functions and way of working is too different from the common lisp, it is taking me a while to figure out and internalize the differences haha.
But both are crazy awesome and fun to tinker with.

Is Lisp Dead?

Yes, I thought it was, but once you get into its ecosystem you see that there are a lot of people working with lisp and maintaining, keeping it alive and always innovating.
It has its own libraries and frameworks, its macros that can modify the language to accept new paradigms and everything! It is not widely known and used like Java for enterprise solutions, but closure is a dialect of lisp that is quite used and known, more than its standard (common lisp).

Conclusion

Lisp isn’t a dead language and it is quite exciting to try out sometimes. It has a community and mature libraries to serious development but not much margin in enterprise application, although it can change (are you a CEO? why not try out lisp?) with companies using it in their software development and developers building more libraries and apps with Lisp.
